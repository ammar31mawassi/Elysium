# Arab Student Struggles: Research Base

This file records the evidence behind Dalili. The purpose is not to prove that Arab students are weak. The point is to show where the system creates friction, and where a focused product can make the first year less confusing.

## Research Snapshot

Arab students are entering Israeli higher education in much larger numbers than before, but the first-year transition remains the critical risk point.

Key evidence:

- The Council for Higher Education reported about **60,600 Arab students** in Israeli higher education in 2021/2022, about **18%** of all students, compared with about **21%** of the population. The same report says Arab student numbers grew by about **133%** since 2009/2010. Source: [CHE 2022/23 data](https://www.che.org.il/wp-content/uploads/2022/10/Press-Release-Collection-of-Data-for-Start-of-Year-202223.pdf).
- The same CHE report says the first year receives the most significant support because first-year success reduces dropout and increases degree completion chances. Source: [CHE 2022/23 data](https://www.che.org.il/wp-content/uploads/2022/10/Press-Release-Collection-of-Data-for-Start-of-Year-202223.pdf).
- An older CHE/CBS-backed report cites first-year non-continuation of **15.5%** among Arab students versus **11%** among Jewish students for students who began in 2009/2010 and did not continue to year two in 2010/2011. Source: [Pluralism and Equal Opportunity in Higher Education](https://che.org.il/wp-content/uploads/2013/03/Pluralism-and-equal-opportunities-in-higher-education-FINAL.pdf).
- A 2020 Israel Democracy Institute and Aharon Institute survey found **22%** of Arab students considered quitting or taking a break, compared with **10%** of Jewish students. It also reported stronger financial and digital strain among Arab students during COVID-era distance learning. Source: [Israel Democracy Institute](https://en.idi.org.il/articles/32628).
- Mansour's 2024 mixed-methods study found that Palestinian Arab citizens of Israel experience academic, physical, social, and cultural disorientation on entering higher education. The study identifies language, age, and finances as structural barriers, and notes that students often turn to one another and form groups for support. Source: [Mansour, 2024](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/03075079.2023.2231019).
- The Open University Arabic support page describes the move from Arabic-language schooling to Hebrew academic study as a radical and complex transition, especially in the first years. Source: [Open University Arabic academic support](https://www.openu.ac.il/ar/support/pages/academic_support.aspx).
- Tel Aviv University's Sawa program validates the intervention model: preparation before the first year, academic/social support, and second/third-year student mentors. Source: [TAU Sawa Arabic page](https://ar-go.tau.ac.il/choose_sawaheb).

## Problem Map

### 1. Language Load

Arab students often move from Arabic-speaking school environments into Hebrew-dominant campuses, while many course readings and technical materials are in English.

Dalili implication:

- Guides must explain Hebrew academic terms in Arabic.
- Content should include templates for emails, office-hour questions, course registration terms, grade appeals, scholarship language, Moodle terms, and exam rules.
- Search must handle Arabic, Hebrew, and English keywords for the same student problem.

### 2. Hidden Curriculum

Students may not know the unwritten rules: how to talk to lecturers, when to ask for help, what a syllabus means, how office hours work, how to choose courses, when to appeal, what counts as normal academic struggle, and where deadlines are hidden.

Dalili implication:

- Every guide should include "what nobody tells you" notes.
- Mentors should be organized around situations, not only fields.
- Onboarding should ask where the student feels uncertain, not only what they study.

### 3. First-Year Disorientation

The first semester combines new geography, new systems, new academic expectations, new social environments, and often the first time living away from home.

Dalili implication:

- The dashboard should prioritize "what matters now" instead of showing a generic app home.
- University pages should include practical campus orientation: offices, contacts, student union, transport, dorms, libraries, calendars, scholarship links, and common mistakes.
- Groups should support first-year belonging, not only course study.

### 4. Financial And Work Pressure

Research points to stronger financial pressure among Arab students. Students may work while studying, commute long distances, or avoid helpful prep programs because they need income.

Dalili implication:

- Scholarship and rights guidance should be first-class.
- The planner should understand work shifts and commute constraints.
- Resources should distinguish "quick action this week" from long explanations.

### 5. Age And Social Gap

Many Arab students begin higher education younger than Jewish peers who often enter after military service. This can affect confidence, independence, campus familiarity, and social belonging.

Dalili implication:

- Mentors should normalize confusion and help students understand campus culture.
- The product tone should avoid shame, panic, or over-motivation.
- First-year guides should be practical, short, and reassuring without being childish.

### 6. Belonging And Identity

Academic research shows Arabic has little presence in much of Israeli academia, and that Arabic alongside Hebrew can support belonging. Source: [ERIC record on Arabic in Israeli higher education](https://eric.ed.gov/?id=EJ1315220).

Dalili implication:

- Arabic is not just a translation setting. Arabic is the default trust layer.
- Hebrew and English appear as tools to navigate university, not as the emotional voice of the app.
- Design should feel like it was made from inside the student experience.

## What Dalili Should Help With

Dalili should reduce:

- Time spent searching scattered information.
- Fear of asking "basic" questions.
- Confusion around Hebrew academic/admin language.
- Missed deadlines.
- Isolation in the first semester.
- Uncertainty about whom to contact.
- Dependence on random WhatsApp groups with unclear reliability.

Dalili should increase:

- Fast access to trustworthy Arabic explanations.
- Student-to-student support.
- Awareness of rights, scholarships, and services.
- Confidence in asking lecturers, secretariats, and advisors.
- Early connection to groups and mentors.
- Persistence through the first semester.

## Evidence Quality Notes

- CHE and CBS-backed data are strong for participation and dropout context, but some dropout numbers are older. They are still useful because newer studies continue to identify first-year transition as a critical issue.
- COVID-era survey data should not be treated as normal-year behavior, but it is useful for showing unequal pressure and digital/financial gaps.
- Mansour 2024 is highly relevant because it directly studies Palestinian Arab undergraduate transition experiences in Israeli higher education.
- University support pages are not neutral research, but they validate that institutions themselves recognize the need for prep, mentoring, language support, and first-year guidance.

## Product Research Questions Still Open

- Which three universities should be supported first?
- Which first-year problems are most painful for students in computer science, nursing, medicine, law, and education?
- What information do Arab students currently get from WhatsApp groups, and what is missing?
- Would students trust mentor profiles with WhatsApp contact, or prefer in-app requests?
- What content should be public without login, and what should require an account?
