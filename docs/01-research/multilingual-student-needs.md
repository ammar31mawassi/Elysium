# Multilingual Student Needs

This document expands the research frame from one student population to all students in Israeli higher education while preserving targeted support for groups that face greater friction.

## Product Position

The product should be universal in access but specific in help.

It serves all students, while personalization changes what each student sees according to:

- Institution and study field.
- Academic year and current moment.
- Preferred interface language.
- Comfort reading and speaking Hebrew, Arabic, and English.
- New immigrant, international, first-generation, commuter, working, reserve-duty, disability, or housing context when voluntarily supplied.
- Help needs rather than ethnicity alone.

The product must not assume that every Arab student struggles in the same way, or that Hebrew-speaking students do not need guidance. It should route support based on actual context.

## Shared Student Problems

### Hidden Curriculum

Many university tasks are understandable only after someone explains the unwritten process: how to read a syllabus, contact a lecturer, choose courses, submit an appeal, find office hours, or decide which office owns a problem.

Product response:

- Situation-based guides.
- University-specific instructions.
- Templates for messages and forms.
- Clear ownership: who to contact, when, and what to prepare.

### Fragmented Information

Official information is spread across university sites, student portals, email, Moodle, student unions, PDFs, social media, and informal WhatsApp groups.

Product response:

- One searchable guide layer with official sources.
- Date and source visibility.
- University and campus filters.
- Human review rather than unverified aggregation.

### Financial And Time Pressure

The National Union of Israeli Students' 2025 survey page reports average monthly working-student income of **NIS 6,291** and average monthly expenses of **NIS 6,879**, a gap of roughly **NIS 588**. A 2026 Knesset Economic Affairs Committee discussion similarly described a structural student deficit of about **NIS 600 per month**. Sources: [NUIS student data](https://www.nuis.co.il/stats/), [Knesset Economic Affairs Committee, 2026](https://main.knesset.gov.il/EN/News/PressReleases/Pages/press19526e.aspx).

Product response:

- Scholarships, rights, benefits, and deadline guidance.
- Commute- and work-aware planning.
- Short answers that identify the next useful action.
- No assumption that students have unlimited time for campus services.

### Belonging And Human Support

Students need people as well as information. Existing institutional programs use mentoring, buddy systems, social support, and preparatory guidance for different populations. Examples include Hebrew University's preparatory mentoring, TAU support for new immigrants, and Hebrew University's international buddy program. Sources: [Hebrew University preparatory programs](https://en.huji.ac.il/preparatory-programs), [TAU support for olim](https://international.tau.ac.il/taui-aliya), [Hebrew University international buddy guide](https://new.huji.ac.il/sites/default/files/mainsite/files/the_hebrew_university_erasmus_guide_version_4.0_2021.pdf).

Product response:

- Approved peer mentors.
- Study, social, course, and transition groups.
- Filters by institution, field, language, and help topic.
- Clear reporting and moderation.

### Disruption And Quit Risk

The NUIS 2025 survey reports that **34% of all respondents considered leaving their studies** and **48% felt the war substantially affected their academic performance**. The rates were higher for several directly affected groups. These are wartime figures and should not be treated as a normal-year baseline, but they show the need for timely guidance, benefits information, and recovery paths. Source: [NUIS Student Survey 2025](https://www.nuis.co.il/student-report-2025/).

Product response:

- Surface relevant rights, accommodations, and institutional contacts.
- Support students returning after interruption.
- Avoid assuming a single uninterrupted semester path.

## Multilingual Friction

Hebrew, Arabic, and English are not merely interface choices. Students regularly encounter official terminology, course material, forms, or conversations in a language different from their preferred language.

Examples:

- Arab students may move from Arabic-language schooling into Hebrew instruction with English readings.
- New immigrants may begin in English or French and transition into Hebrew programs. TAU explicitly offers academic integration tracks for new immigrants that begin in English or French and graduate in Hebrew. Source: [TAU support for olim](https://international.tau.ac.il/taui-aliya).
- International students may study in English but still need Hebrew for practical campus life.
- Hebrew-speaking students may need English academic vocabulary and source-reading support.

Product response:

- English, Hebrew, and Arabic must each support complete navigation and core workflows.
- Search should match equivalent terms across all three languages.
- Guides should preserve official terminology in its source language and explain it in the user's chosen language.
- Language preference must not determine which resources, mentors, or groups a student can access.
- RTL and LTR layouts must be equally complete.

## Priority Segments

The broad audience does not remove prioritization. Early interviews and content should intentionally include:

- First-year and preparatory-year students.
- Arab students crossing from Arabic schooling into Hebrew-dominant academia.
- New immigrants and international students.
- First-generation students.
- Working students and long-distance commuters.
- Students with accessibility needs.
- Students returning after reserve duty or disruption.

## Research Implications

- Interview by situation and segment, not by asking only generic student questions.
- Test every major workflow in English, Hebrew, and Arabic.
- Track whether content coverage is equal across languages.
- Preserve the original Arab-student research as a depth advantage, not as the product's only audience.
- Do not claim that one universal workflow solves every population's needs.

## Open Questions

- Which student segment should be the first launch cohort?
- Which university provides the best pilot environment?
- Should the default interface follow device language or an explicit first-screen choice?
- Which guides must be fully human-written in all three languages before launch?
- How should mentors declare language comfort and availability?
- Which official terms need a three-language glossary first?
